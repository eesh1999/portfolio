01 / The Context

The 10th Grade Bottleneck.

In India, the transition from 10th to 11th grade is critical. Students must choose a "Stream" (Science, Commerce, Arts) that dictates their entire future. Currently, this decision is based on fear and rote memorization marks, not actual aptitude.

Students are paralyzed by choice ("Netflix Paralysis"), while parents block non-traditional careers because they lack "safe" financial data. The result? A generation of students who have book knowledge but zero real-world context.

The HMW Statement

How might we help students visualize and physically test-drive future career paths based on their skills, making it easier (and safer) for them to decide?

02 / Research & Process

Digging Deeper: The 5 Whys.

I didn't just want to know what they wanted, but why they were scared. I conducted 10 student interviews and 5 parent interviews using the "5 Whys" framework.

01

The Trust Gap

Parents don't hate Design; they hate Risk. They said, "I don't know if this job pays well." They needed data, not passion.

02

The Autonomy Issue

Students feel suffocated by "Career Counseling" that feels like school. If it's mandatory, they reject it.

03

Tangibility

"Watching a video isn't the same as doing it." Students feel like imposters in workplaces. They need a "Uniform" to feel professional.

From Chaos to Clarity

04 / Design System

Visual Language: "Construction Chic."

06 / The Secondary User

Designing for the "Gatekeeper."

If the parent says "No," the product fails. I designed a passive Parent Notification System to build trust without being intrusive.

Instead of just seeing "Grades," parents receive Scope Articles and Progress Updates. This educates them on the viability of non-traditional careers, solving the "Trust Gap" identified in the research.

06 / Business Viability

Who pays for the box?

A physical "Loot Crate" model is expensive logistically. To ensure scalability and affordability, the business model relies on a Tri-Party Ecosystem.

$

The Revenue Model

Freemium + Transactional.

  • App (Free): Low barrier to entry. Acts as a lead magnet.
  • Kit (Paid): Parents pay per "Experience." This lowers risk compared to paying a massive annual counselor fee.
%

The Cost Subsidy

Corporate Sponsorship.

The "Chef Kit" isn't just a product; it's a marketing channel. Brands (e.g., Masterchef) subsidize the cost of the box for brand placement.

‚≠Ø

Scalability (B2B)

School Partnerships.

By selling B2B to schools, we ship "Classroom Packs" (30 kits at once), reducing logistics costs by ~40%.

The Outcomes

Testing with 15 students and 5 parents revealed a clear divergence between "Passive Watching" (App) and "Active Doing" (Kit).

80% vs 40%

Retention Rate (Kit vs. Video)

The Insight: While 60% of students dropped off during the passive "Video Trailers," 12 out of 15 students completed the entire physical "Knife Skills" module.

The Insight: Before the app, parent questions were financial ("Does this pay?"). After seeing the 'Scope Reports' and video logs, questions shifted to aptitude.

08 / Edge Cases & Friction

Designing for the Margins.

A product isn't finished until it handles failure gracefully. I stress-tested the "Happy Path" to find potential pitfalls.

The "Happy Failure" State

Scenario: A student tries the "Coding Kit," hates it, and gets a low aptitude score.

The UX Pivot: Standard apps show red text ("Low Score"). I reframed this as a Financial Win: "You just saved ‚Çπ8 Lakhs on a degree you wouldn't enjoy."

Pivot: No Facial Scanning

Concept: Initially considered facial recognition for sentiment.

Why I Killed It: Privacy risks with minors. Switched to Voice Tone Analysis which is less invasive and more reliable on low-end hardware.

Accessibility: Audio First

Constraint: Users cannot touch the screen while their hands are covered in clay, grease, or food.

The Solution: Implemented "Wave to Pause" (gesture control) and Auto-Subtitles for noisy workshops.
